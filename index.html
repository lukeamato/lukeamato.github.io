However, it will be important to have a basic understanding of what the 'iron condor' strategy is. I will offer two explanations now, one for those who have traded options before and one for those who are less familiar. For those who have traded options before, the iron condor strategy is where you sell two credit spreads -- one bearish call credit spread above the underlying and one bullish put credit spread below the underlyng, with the goal of having the underlying remain within the bounds of your condor by expiration. For those who are less familiar with options, the iron condor strategy can be described as making a bet that the stock price of a company will stay within certain bounds that you set by a certain date. For example, let's say I have a company such as Amazon, and its stock is trading at \$3500 per share. Then, I could make a bet that Amazon stock will not rise above \$4000, and will not fall below \$3000, by a certain date, let's say one month from now. If I make this bet, I will get paid a certain amount of money at the time I make the bet, with the risk that if Amazon does in fact move above or below the bounds I have bet on, I will lose all of that premium I was originally paid, and perhaps more.

If this concept is still a bit unclear, here is a phenomenal video made by Chris from projectfinance, one of my favorite Youtube channels. As Chris says in the video, lots of investors love the iron condor options strategy because it is market-neutral, it is limited-risk (meaning you know your maximum risk when entering the trade), and it makes you money when the underlying stock DOES NOT experience large price movements. This strategy exists in contrast to what many people, especially new investors, think happens in the stock market, that prices are extremely volatile and you can lose all your money in an instant, or alternatively, that you can double your money in no time. Neither of these preconceptions are realistic. Our whole goal here is to analyze the weekly price movements of the SPY S&P 500 ETF. However, the data as it exists only describes what the prices were at the end of each week. To continue, we need to create a column to describe the weekly return of SPY. We can get this data by taking (adj close from week x+1 - adj close from week x) / (adj close from week x). Seems simple enough, but there is one kink to think about. The final row of the DataFrame has no "week x+1", because it is the last row. Instead, I will just calculate up to the second to last week and drop the last row from the table afterward. Here we go:

for rowNumber, row in data.iterrows():

    #Calculate the weeklyReturn for each row
    data['weeklyReturn'][rowNumber] = (data['Adj Close'][rowNumber + 1] - \

IV. Hypothesis Testing & Machine Learning

For my purposes, I will not be needing any significant machine learning (sorry). Instead, I can focus on the basis: finding both a 90% and 95% confidence interval on the weekly returns, and trying to see if there is a significant trend in volatility over time.

The purpose of the confidence intervals: If we can find an interval within which we expect, with confidence, SPY to stay each and every week, we could use those expected moves to help place both sides of our iron condor options strategy. For example, if SPY is at \$400, and we find that the 95% confident interval for weekly price movement is [-5%, 5%], then we can bet with fairly high confidence that SPY will not move above \$420 or below \$380 within the next week. Then, we can make a bet and start churning in profit.

Vital to note, however, is that if we continue making these bets every single week of the year, there WILL be weeks in which our bet is incorrect and SPY moves above or below our interval. For that reason, the iron condor takes more than just confidence. Specifically, a statistics-based or probabilistic iron condor strategy would be foolish if not to include a loss-management policy. Beyond the scope of this analysis, but there are ways to further hedge against our iron condor position in the case that SPY makes a large move in one particular week. I hope to further explore this strategy, possibly creating an automated trading bot with loss-management, in the future. For now, let's focus on these confidence intervals and regression to see what is going on with the data:

V. Communication of Insight Attained


Now, I have talked through a few of the insights attained throghout the first four sections of the data science pipeline, but this fifth and final section is a good time to reflect and summarize what we have learned through doing this analysis. I set out at the beginning of this analysis to start building a probabilistic iron condor trading strategy on the SPY S&P 500 ETF underlying asset. I wanted to do so using historical data to determine confidence intervals on the average weekly move. The hope was that these confidence intervals could serve as the boundaries of a profit-bearing iron condor strategy. After completing the analysis, we found that, even with high 97.5% confidence, the expected interval was still quite small: [-.01%, .7%]. This actually tells us quite an interesting fact, that it is unlikely for SPY to have a negative move most weeks, or over a 1% move upward.

In terms of our basic iron condor strategy, this means we could set up our thresholds each week at about -.5% and 1.5%, having a good feeling that SPY will not move outside those bounds. As we talked about before, there will certainly be weeks where SPY does rise above or drop below our boundaries, and that is where we need to incorporate more mature loss and risk management strategies. That analysis will be reserved for another day.

The last two things to note before I end the communication of my findings is that we need to note both the trend of volatility over time and the true profit we would be gaining by setting our weekly iron condor bounds at those theoretical points (-.5% and 1.5%). First of all, the confidence intervals we found we meant to serve the purpose of bounds outside of which we could feel confident setting our iron condor. We achieved that, but what if the iron condor at that level is not profitable? For example, what if making a bet that SPY will not go up by 1.5% this week, is actually a fairly obvious assumption in the market and it will not yield much profit? In that case, we would have to either go with intervals of lower confidence, or go back to the drawing board. At a future date, I plan to begin pulling options price data from TD Ameritrade (my brokerage), and truly start to analyze what the profitability would be of this probabilistic iron condor strategy. If the strategy, at our 95% confidence interval, yields less than an 8% gain per year, we would be better off dropping this all together and simply investing in SPY itself!
